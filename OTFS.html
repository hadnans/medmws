01 · Concept

The Delay–Doppler Domain

Classical multicarrier systems like OFDM place information symbols in the time-frequency plane. This works well when the channel is slowly varying — but in high-mobility scenarios (vehicular, LEO satellite, mmWave), rapid Doppler shifts destroy subcarrier orthogonality and cause inter-carrier interference (ICI) that is expensive to mitigate.

OTFS (proposed by Hadani et al., 2017) takes a fundamentally different approach: it multiplexes data in the delay–Doppler (DD) domain, which is the natural representation of the doubly-dispersive channel. Each physical propagation path is characterized by a delay τ and a Doppler shift ν, and OTFS treats this sparsity directly.

In the DD domain the channel appears as a sparse, stable, and separable 2-D impulse response, regardless of the vehicle speed. An information symbol placed at a DD grid point (l, k) experiences a simple multiplicative gain from each path — there is no ICI because the entire Doppler spread is captured in the domain, not fought against.

Core Principle

OTFS exploits the fact that the wireless channel, while appearing time-varying and dispersive in the time-frequency domain, is time-invariant and compact in the delay-Doppler domain. The channel's complexity is absorbed into the domain transform, not into the equalizer.

The DD Channel Model

A doubly-dispersive channel with P propagation paths has an input-output relation in continuous time:

y(t) = ∑ₚ hₚ · x(t − τₚ) · e^{j2πνₚ(t−τₚ)}
Continuous-time doubly-dispersive channel · P paths

where hₚ, τₚ, νₚ are the complex gain, delay, and Doppler shift of the p-th path respectively. OTFS maps this to a 2-D convolution in the DD domain:

Y[k,l] = ∑_{k',l'} H[k−k', l−l'] · X[k',l'] + W[k,l]
Discrete DD input-output relation · M×N grid

Here X[k,l] is a symbol placed on the DD grid (size N×M), H[k,l] is the DD channel response (sparse!), and W[k,l] is additive noise. This 2-D convolution structure enables powerful structured equalization.

Grid Parameters

The DD grid has N Doppler bins (index k) and M delay bins (index l). The Doppler resolution is Δν = 1/(NTΔf) and the delay resolution is Δτ = 1/(MΔf) where Δf is the subcarrier spacing. Total frame duration is T_f = NT/M samples.

The ISFFT / SFFT Transform Pair

OTFS uses a pair of 2-D symplectic Fourier transforms to move between the DD and time-frequency domains:

X_TF[n,m] = ∑_{k=0}^{N−1} ∑_{l=0}^{M−1} X[k,l] · e^{j2π(nk/N − ml/M)}
Inverse Symplectic Finite Fourier Transform (ISFFT)
DD → Time-Frequency mapping at the transmitter
Y[k,l] = ∑_{n=0}^{N−1} ∑_{m=0}^{M−1} Y_TF[n,m] · e^{−j2π(nk/N − ml/M)}
Symplectic Finite Fourier Transform (SFFT)
Time-Frequency → DD mapping at the receiver
02 · Architecture

Transmitter & Receiver Block Diagrams

Fig 1 — OTFS Transmitter Block Diagram

The transmitter chain works as follows:

  • 1
    QAM Mapping: Information bits are mapped to complex symbols X[k,l] placed on the N×M delay-Doppler grid. Each grid point corresponds to a unique (Doppler-bin, delay-bin) pair.
  • 2
    ISFFT: The 2-D Inverse Symplectic Fourier Transform converts X[k,l] from the DD plane to the time-frequency plane X_TF[n,m]. This is the key operation — it "spreads" each DD symbol across all time-frequency resources, earning full diversity.
  • 3
    Heisenberg Transform (Pulse Shaping): A transmit pulse g_tx(t) (often a rectangular or root-raised cosine pulse) modulates the TF coefficients to produce the continuous-time signal s(t). In the rectangular case this reduces to a Gabor expansion.
  • 4
    Cyclic Prefix / Zero Padding: A CP or ZP of length ≥ maximum delay spread is appended to each block to prevent inter-block interference and to facilitate simple frequency-domain equalization.
  • 5
    DAC / RF Upconversion: The baseband signal is converted to analog and upconverted to the carrier frequency for transmission — identical to any standard RF front-end.
Fig 2 — OTFS Receiver Block Diagram
  • 1
    RF Downconversion / ADC: The received RF signal is downconverted to baseband and digitized. This is the standard front-end, identical to OFDM receivers.
  • 2
    CP/ZP Removal: The guard interval is stripped, yielding the received block r(t) in the time domain.
  • 3
    Wigner Transform (Cross-Ambiguity Function): The receive pulse g_rx(t) is cross-correlated with r(t) to extract the time-frequency representation Y_TF[n,m]. This is the matched-filter step and the dual of the Heisenberg transform at the transmitter.
  • 4
    SFFT: The 2-D Symplectic Fourier Transform maps Y_TF[n,m] back to the delay-Doppler domain to obtain Y[k,l], which contains the transmitted symbols convolved with the channel's DD impulse response.
  • 5
    DD Domain Equalization: Since the channel is a sparse 2-D convolution in the DD domain, equalization is performed there. Common algorithms include MMSE, Message Passing (MP), and iterative turbo equalization.
Rectangular vs Ideal Pulses

Using rectangular pulses (practically implementable) leads to the CP-OTFS or OFDM-based implementation, where the ISFFT/SFFT reduce to an outer 2-D DFT around the conventional OFDM IFFT/FFT. Using ideal bi-orthogonal pulses achieves the theoretically clean input-output relationship but is physically non-realizable — making CP-OTFS the dominant implementation choice.

03 · Implementation

Implementation Details

OTFS can be implemented in two architecturally distinct ways — as a standalone waveform or as a precoding layer on top of OFDM — making it highly backward-compatible with existing 5G NR infrastructure.

⊞

Standalone OTFS

The full Heisenberg/Wigner pipeline with a custom pulse shaping filter. Achieves maximum diversity but requires dedicated hardware, particularly for non-rectangular pulses. Research-grade hardware platforms (e.g., NI USRP, Xilinx RFSoC) are commonly used.

≋

CP-OTFS (OFDM-based)

An ISFFT precoder added before the standard OFDM IFFT, and an SFFT post-processor after the FFT at the receiver. Fully compatible with 4G/5G NR OFDM hardware. The most practical and widely studied form.

Equalization in the DD Domain

Equalization is the most computationally intensive part of OTFS reception. Three main approaches exist:

MMSE Equalizer

Minimum Mean Square Error linear equalizer. Uses knowledge of H[k,l] to jointly equalize in the DD domain. Complexity is O((NM)³) without structure exploitation but can be reduced using the banded or circulant structure of the channel matrix.

Message Passing (MP)

Iterative belief propagation on the factor graph of the DD channel. Exploits channel sparsity — if the channel has P paths, the graph is very sparse, reducing complexity to O(P·NM) per iteration. Widely used in literature.

Turbo / Iterative

Joint equalization-decoding that passes soft information between the equalizer and channel decoder iteratively. Achieves near-ML performance in high-Doppler environments by leveraging the coding gain alongside DD-domain equalization.

DNN / Learning-based

Deep neural networks trained offline to approximate optimal MMSE/MAP equalization. ResNet and LSTM architectures have been proposed. Handles fractional Doppler and non-ideal pulse shapes without explicit channel modeling.

Channel Estimation in OTFS

Channel estimation exploits the sparse, stable nature of the DD channel. A single impulse pilot placed at a known DD grid point (k_p, l_p) with sufficient guard space allows estimation of all P path delays and Dopplers from its echo in the received DD frame. The guard region must be:

l_guard ≥ l_max     (delay guard)
k_guard ≥ k_max     (Doppler guard)
Guard requirements for pilot-based DD channel estimation

where l_max and k_max are the maximum delay and Doppler bins of the channel. This is extremely pilot-efficient compared to OFDM, which needs pilots distributed in both time and frequency due to the time-varying nature of the channel in that domain.

04 · Applications

Applications & Use Cases

OTFS's high-mobility robustness makes it a candidate waveform across a spectrum of scenarios where OFDM begins to fail — from vehicular networks to satellite links to ISAC systems.

Vehicle-to-Everything (V2X)

Highway vehicular communication at 120–250 km/h produces Doppler spreads of 1–5 kHz at sub-6GHz bands. OTFS maintains bit-error-rate performance orders of magnitude better than OFDM in such environments, enabling reliable URLLC for autonomous driving safety messages.

LEO Satellite Broadband

LEO satellites (500–2000 km altitude) orbit at ~7.5 km/s, producing Doppler shifts up to 48 kHz at Ka-band. OTFS handles this extreme mobility without per-subcarrier compensation loops required by OFDM, making it attractive for Starlink-class broadband access.

Integrated Sensing & Communication (ISAC)

OTFS is naturally suited to ISAC/JCAS because the delay-Doppler frame directly encodes target range and velocity. By correlating the received signal with the transmitted OTFS frame, range-velocity maps can be estimated while simultaneously communicating — with no dedicated radar waveform needed.

High-Speed Rail (HSR)

Modern high-speed trains reach 350+ km/h, causing rapid time variation that severely degrades OFDM in the 2.6 GHz TD-LTE/5G NR bands. OTFS provides a stable, high-throughput link in these scenarios with simpler channel tracking requirements.

mmWave & THz Communications

At mmWave (24–100 GHz), even pedestrian mobility (5 km/h) produces significant normalized Doppler. OTFS coupled with massive MIMO beamforming resolves mobility-induced beam misalignment more gracefully than OFDM, enabling robust 5G/6G mmWave links for mobile users.

Underwater Acoustic (UWA)

Underwater channels exhibit severe spreading — delay spreads of 100s of ms and Doppler spreads due to surface motion. OTFS's DD domain perspective maps perfectly onto this doubly-dispersive UWA channel, and experimental results show significant gains over OFDM-based UWA systems.

05 · Assessment

Advantages & Limitations

✦ Advantages

Full Diversity: Each symbol experiences all delay-Doppler diversity branches of the channel, achieving the maximum diversity order P (number of paths). Compared to OFDM where individual subcarriers see only frequency-flat fading, OTFS delivers dramatically lower BER in multipath-Doppler channels.
Doppler Robustness: The DD domain naturally captures all Doppler shifts, eliminating inter-carrier interference (ICI) that plagues OFDM at high velocities. No special ICI cancellation or pilot overhead is needed per subcarrier.
Compact Channel Representation: The DD channel response is sparse (P paths → P spikes) and time-invariant within the frame duration, dramatically simplifying channel estimation and equalization compared to the full N×M time-varying matrix in OFDM.
Pilot Efficiency: A single DD pilot with guards suffices for full channel estimation, regardless of frame size. OFDM requires a 2D pilot grid scaled with the coherence bandwidth and coherence time.
ISAC Synergy: The delay-Doppler frame is a native range-velocity map, enabling dual-function waveforms with no extra overhead. This is a major advantage for 6G ISAC systems.
OFDM Compatibility: CP-OTFS adds only a 2-D DFT precoder/post-processor around existing OFDM hardware, enabling evolutionary (not revolutionary) deployment in 5G NR networks.

✦ Limitations

Receiver Complexity: Full MMSE equalization in the DD domain has cubic complexity O((NM)³) without structural exploitation. While MP reduces this, complexity is still higher than OFDM's per-subcarrier one-tap equalizer, especially for large frame sizes.
Fractional Doppler: When a target's Doppler shift does not fall on an exact DD grid point, energy leaks into neighboring bins. This "fractional Doppler" problem causes inter-symbol interference and degrades performance, requiring windowing or oversampling in the Doppler dimension.
High PAPR: Like OFDM, OTFS waveforms exhibit high Peak-to-Average Power Ratio (~10–12 dB), requiring power-backed-off or linearized power amplifiers, increasing hardware cost and reducing power efficiency at the transmitter.
Latency: OTFS spreads each symbol across the entire N×M frame in both time and frequency. This full spreading means the receiver must collect an entire frame before any symbol can be decoded, imposing a minimum latency of one full OTFS frame duration.
Non-Rectangular Pulse Complexity: Theoretically optimal bi-orthogonal pulse shaping filters are non-realizable (require infinite support). Practical rectangular pulses introduce interference in the DD domain that must be carefully managed.
Standardization Gap: As of 2025, OTFS has not been incorporated into 3GPP 5G NR standards (Rel-17/18). Practical deployment awaits PHY-layer standardization, ecosystem maturation, and demonstration at scale — leaving OFDM dominant in deployed networks.

Research Frontier (2024–2025)

Active research directions include: OTFS-NOMA (combining DD multiplexing with power-domain NOMA), RIS-assisted OTFS for controllable channel paths, OTFS with massive MIMO for spatial-DD joint processing, and ODDM (Orthogonal Delay-Doppler Modulation) — a cousin waveform using delay-Doppler domain OFDM with additional orthogonality guarantees under ideal pulse conditions.
